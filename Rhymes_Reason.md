# AFTERWORD TO THE

Many years ago, when I was first asked to teach a col- lege course in poetry writing, I was of several minds as to how it might be done. It seemed to me that much poetry then in vogue was artless and merely personal, and so I was tempted to inform my aspirant poets that they were about to share an art with John Milton, that master of form and of great themes. On second thoughts, I saw that it was not my business to be bossy and daunting, and that I should find a way to be of use. What I did, in a spirit of compromise, was to devote our first two class-meetings to some elements of poetic form.

## We studied the variations of the pentameter in

## Tennyson's great poem "Tithonus," and read a number of other poems which might have been inspiring in themselves, had I not grimly plundered them for trochee and dactyl. One or two students dropped the course, which gave our class a more manageable size; yet I was sorry to lose them, as I might not have done if had been available as a text for our course. For many students since, John Hollander's brilliant and engaging book has eased the encounter with prosody.

## In the making of any poem, however grave or

138 Afterword to the Fourth Edition earnest, there is an element of play, as Robert Frost implies in the closing lines of "Two Tramps in Mud

## Time":

### Only when love and need are one

### And the work is play for mortal stakes

### Is the deed ever really done

### For Heaven and the future's sakes.

In Frost's poem, the actual deed in question is the splitting of firewood on an April morning, yet as so often in Frost, something is suggested about the art of poetry. To embark on the making of a poem is a passionate gamble, a "playing for mortal stakes"; and something else is called into play at the same time-- what Clive James has called the "subservient impulse" of technique. Through the interplay of utterance and of formal technique, the poem gradually comes to be.

This book's wit and inventive spirit, its self-describing embodiments of form, now offer the beginning poet a happy chance to discover the technician in himself.
